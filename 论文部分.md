#### 2.4. Physics-informed Hybrid Active Learning Framework
This study investigates the performance of machine learning algorithms on a limited dataset of 146 samples. To achieve efficient learning and accelerate model performance improvement under small-data conditions, we introduce a customized hybrid active learning framework. This approach prioritizes labeling unexplored parameter combinations with the highest information entropy and physical significance. The proposed framework integrates a weighted committee-based uncertainty formulation, a physics-informed boundary weighting mechanism, and a K-Means diversity sampling strategy, thereby ensuring a balanced exploration-exploitation trade-off within the laser powder bed fusion (LPBF) process space.

##### 2.4.1. Weighted Committee Uncertainty (WCU) for Multi-objective Quantification
To rigorously quantify the epistemic uncertainty of the predictive models, a Query-by-Committee (QBC) approach was employed using a heterogeneous ensemble comprising three top-performing models: Random Forest, XGBoost, and LightGBM. Let $M=3$ denote the number of models in the committee, and $T=3$ denote the number of target variables (relative density, impedance, and corrosion current density $J_{corr}$). 

To eliminate the dimensional discrepancies among different physical targets, the prediction output of each model for a given candidate sample $x$ was first standardized using Z-score normalization. Subsequently, the standardized variance across the committee was calculated as the uncertainty metric for each target:
$$
\sigma_t^2(x) = \frac{1}{M} \sum_{m=1}^{M} \left( \tilde{y}_{m,t}(x) - \mu_t(x) \right)^2
$$
where $\tilde{y}_{m,t}(x)$ represents the standardized prediction of model $m$ for target $t$, and $\mu_t(x)$ is the mean prediction of the committee for that target. To synthesize a global uncertainty score that aligns with the material design priorities, a Weighted Committee Uncertainty ($U_{WCU}$) was formulated by assigning predefined domain-knowledge weights to each target:
$$
U_{WCU}(x) = \sum_{t=1}^{T} w_t \cdot \sigma_t^2(x)
$$
where the weight vector was empirically set as $w_{density}=0.5$, $w_{impedance}=0.3$, and $w_{J_{corr}}=0.2$. A higher $U_{WCU}$ indicates greater disagreement among the models, highlighting regions where the algorithm is most "uncertain" and thus possesses higher informational value.

##### 2.4.2. Physics-informed Boundary Weighting Mechanism
In addition to model uncertainty, the active learning strategy incorporates physical priors by emphasizing phase-transition and saturation boundaries in the LPBF process. A boundary weighting function, $W(x)$, was introduced based on the volumetric energy density ($E$) of the samples. The parameter space was stratified into three bins: Low ($E < 81 \text{ J/mm}^3$), Mid ($81 \le E \le 111 \text{ J/mm}^3$), and High ($E > 111 \text{ J/mm}^3$). 

To heavily sample the critical transition zones (where material properties change non-linearly) and the extreme processing limits, the weighting function was defined as:
$$
W(x) =
\begin{cases}
1.3, & \text{if } x \in \text{Mid} \text{ and } \left( |E(x) - 80| \le 3 \text{ or } |E(x) - 110| \le 3 \right) \\
1.2, & \text{if } x \in \text{High} \text{ and } E(x) \ge E_{90\%} \\
1.0, & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
$$
where $E_{90\%}$ represents the 90th percentile of the energy density within the High bin. Consequently, the final Acquisition Function, termed the Priority Score ($S(x)$), is defined as the product of the model uncertainty and the physical prior weight:
$$
S(x) = U_{WCU}(x) \times W(x)
$$

##### 2.4.3. Stratified Quota and Diversity Sampling
Relying solely on the maximum Priority Score often leads to selecting highly redundant samples clustered in a narrow process window. To avoid this, a K-Means diversity sampling strategy coupled with stratified quotas was executed. In each active learning iteration, a fixed base quota ($q$) was assigned to each energy density bin to ensure a global distribution of experimental resources.

Within each bin, an exploration buffer was created by selecting the top $2q$ candidate samples ranked by $S(x)$. These $2q$ candidates were then clustered into $q$ distinct clusters ($C_1, C_2, \dots, C_q$) in the 4-dimensional process parameter space using the K-Means algorithm. Finally, a greedy selection was applied within each cluster to pick the most informative representative:
$$
x^*_k = \operatorname*{argmax}_{x \in C_k} S(x), \quad \text{for } k = 1, \dots, q
$$
This hybrid acquisition process was iteratively executed for up to 5 rounds. A dynamic early-stopping criterion was embedded, terminating the loop if the performance gains in $R^2$, MAE, and RMSE over two consecutive rounds fell below 1%, 3%, and 3%, respectively. This rigorously designed strategy significantly enhanced the prediction accuracy and robustness under highly constrained experimental budgets.
